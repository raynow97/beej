Heat & the body

Why your testes evolved to hang outside.

By the editors · 8 min read · April 2026

It is, when you stop to think about it, an odd piece of design. Most of the body's important organs are tucked safely inside, behind ribs, walls of muscle, layers of skin. The testes are not. They sit outside, exposed, in a soft sac that hangs in the open air. There must be a reason.

There is. The testes are temperature-sensitive in a way most other organs aren't. Spermatogenesis — the process by which sperm cells mature, which takes about seventy-four days from start to finish — works best a few degrees below the body's core temperature of 37°C. The optimal range is somewhere between 33°C and 35°C.

If the testes were inside the body, that wouldn't be possible. So evolution moved them outside, into a sac of skin that loosens in heat and tightens in cold. A small, simple piece of biological climate control, refined over millions of years.

The cremaster muscle, briefly.

If you've ever stepped into a cold shower and felt a familiar tightening, you've felt the cremaster muscle at work. It pulls the testes closer to the body in cold weather, conserving warmth. In hot weather it relaxes, letting them hang lower and further from the body's heat. The scrotum itself is corrugated for surface area — more skin to radiate heat through.

There's also a clever piece of plumbing called the pampiniform plexus — a network of veins that wraps around the testicular artery, cooling the warm arterial blood on its way in by exchanging heat with the cooler venous blood on its way out. A counter-current heat exchanger, the same principle that lets a tuna swim in cold water with warm muscles.

For most of human history this was sufficient. The body was upright, mostly outdoors, rarely in close contact with hot surfaces for long. Modern life has changed all three.

Where it goes wrong.

Several common, harmless-seeming features of modern life raise scrotal temperature in measurable ways. Long sitting compresses the scrotum against the thighs and the chair, blocking airflow and trapping heat. Tight underwear and skinny jeans hold the testes against the body, where they pick up the body's warmth. Two-wheelers — particularly the motorcycle culture of urban India — place the rider's groin against a warm seat for the entire commute.

Laptops on laps. Hot showers and hammams. Indian summers in general. None of these are catastrophes individually. The problem is that they stack. A typical office worker in Mumbai has a long sit, a hot commute, tight clothing, and a warm climate, all stacked on the same body, every day.

What the body does about it.

It tries. The cremaster relaxes, the scrotum drops, blood flow shifts. But there's a limit to what the body can compensate for, especially when the heat exposure is sustained for hours rather than minutes.

This is where small, conscious adjustments help. Standing up every hour. Loose cotton underwear. Avoiding the laptop on the lap. Not sitting on the bike seat through a four-hour traffic jam. None of these are revolutionary; all of them help.
